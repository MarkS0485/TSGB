# Clarke & Dawe - "The Inertia Inquiry"
## A Three-Part Investigation into the Great British Blackout

*Two chairs. No set. One country without power.*

---

# PART ONE: "The Lights Go Out"

*INT. A NONDESCRIPT ROOM. CLARKE SITS OPPOSITE DAWE. CLARKE HAS A VERY LARGE FOLDER. THE FOLDER IS ENTIRELY DARK INSIDE.*

---

**DAWE:** Now, you're the Chief Executive of the National Energy System Operator.

**CLARKE:** I am, yes. NESO. Formerly NGESO. Before that, part of National Grid. We've been restructured several times. We find it helps.

**DAWE:** Helps with what?

**CLARKE:** Helps with accountability. Specifically, distributing it to a point where it becomes mathematically undetectable.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** The lights went out.

**CLARKE:** There was an unscheduled demand-side reconnection event, yes.

**DAWE:** The lights went out across most of England and Wales.

**CLARKE:** Across a *significant* portion of the demand portfolio, yes.

**DAWE:** For eleven hours.

**CLARKE:** For an operationally notable duration, yes. Though I'd point out that eleven hours is a very manageable figure if you express it as a fraction of a year.

**DAWE:** It's about 0.1% of a year.

**CLARKE:** Excellent. So statistically, the grid was available 99.9% of the time. That's actually rather good.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** Why did the lights go out?

**CLARKE:** The frequency fell below operational tolerance.

**DAWE:** Why did the frequency fall?

**CLARKE:** The inertia was insufficient to arrest the rate of change of frequency following the initiating event.

**DAWE:** And what was the initiating event?

**CLARKE:** A wind farm turned off.

**DAWE:** One wind farm.

**CLARKE:** One significant wind farm, yes. Quite a large one. Off the coast. They had a technical issue.

**DAWE:** And that took down the grid.

**CLARKE:** It initiated a cascading sequence of -

**DAWE:** One wind farm.

**CLARKE:** One *anchor* wind farm, in a low-inertia operating window, on a mild afternoon in spring when the synchronous fleet was at a historic minimum. The conditions were quite specific.

**DAWE:** How specific?

**CLARKE:** We were operating at approximately 98 gigavolt-ampere-seconds of system inertia.

**DAWE:** Is that good?

**CLARKE:** It's a number. It's *our* number. We chose it. FRCR 2025 certified it as acceptable.

**DAWE:** What was the number in, say, 2016?

**CLARKE:** Around 260.

*Long pause.*

**DAWE:** So you had 260. And you were running it at 98.

**CLARKE:** We had *procured additional dynamic containment services* to compensate for the reduction in synchronous mass.

**DAWE:** What does that mean?

**CLARKE:** It means we paid batteries to react very quickly when things started going wrong.

**DAWE:** Did they?

**CLARKE:** They reacted beautifully. Technically speaking, the response was within specification.

**DAWE:** But the lights went out.

**CLARKE:** The lights went out *before* the response arrived, yes. There's a small timing issue with the approach.

**DAWE:** How small?

**CLARKE:** Well. Physics is quite fast. And our procurement cycle is quarterly.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** So the batteries responded correctly, on time, to a grid that had already failed.

**CLARKE:** To a grid that was *in the process* of failing, yes. The response arrived into what we're calling a dynamic transition.

**DAWE:** A blackout.

**CLARKE:** An unscheduled demand-side reconnection event.

**DAWE:** You keep saying that.

**CLARKE:** We worked quite hard on the wording.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** When did you know the grid was going to fail?

**CLARKE:** The instruments detected the frequency deviation at approximately T-zero.

**DAWE:** When did your response arrive?

**CLARKE:** At approximately T-plus-250-milliseconds. Which is, by any measure, extremely fast.

**DAWE:** And how long did it take the grid to fail?

**CLARKE:** About 80 milliseconds.

*Very long pause.*

**DAWE:** So you were 170 milliseconds late.

**CLARKE:** We were within our contracted response window.

**DAWE:** To a grid that was already gone.

**CLARKE:** We honoured the contract. That's rather the point of having contracts.

*Clarke nods, satisfied.*

# PART TWO: "The Synthetic Inertia"

*INT. THE SAME NONDESCRIPT ROOM. DAWE NOW HAS HIS OWN FOLDER. IT IS ALSO EMPTY.*

---

**DAWE:** Now. You were the Head of Frequency Policy at NESO during the period leading up to the event.

**CLARKE:** I was Head of Frequency Risk and Control Strategy, yes. There's a distinction.

**DAWE:** What's the distinction?

**CLARKE:** Control Strategy sounds more proactive. Risk implies we knew there was one.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** There was a report. FRCR 2025.

**CLARKE:** A consultation document, yes. A very thorough one. Fifty-three pages. Excellent charts.

**DAWE:** It proposed lowering the minimum inertia requirement to 102 gigavolt-ampere-seconds.

**CLARKE:** It proposed that, yes. And gave very good reasons.

**DAWE:** What were the reasons?

**CLARKE:** It was cheaper.

**DAWE:** That was the reason?

**CLARKE:** We framed it more technically than that. But yes, fundamentally, real inertia - actual spinning steel - costs money to procure and keep on the network. Whereas synthetic inertia -

**DAWE:** What's synthetic inertia?

**CLARKE:** It's a battery that pretends to be a flywheel.

**DAWE:** Does it work?

**CLARKE:** It works excellently. Under normal conditions. In steady state. When nothing is happening.

**DAWE:** And when something is happening?

**CLARKE:** It measures the frequency, calculates a response, and injects power.

**DAWE:** How long does that take?

**CLARKE:** 250 milliseconds, approximately.

**DAWE:** And real inertia?

**CLARKE:** Real inertia responds instantaneously. Before measurement. Before calculation. Before anything.

**DAWE:** Before measurement.

**CLARKE:** It's physics. It doesn't wait to be asked.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** So synthetic inertia is a thing that imitates real inertia, but slower.

**CLARKE:** It imitates the *outputs* of real inertia, yes. Under specified conditions.

**DAWE:** What about the inputs?

**CLARKE:** I'm sorry?

**DAWE:** Real inertia - the spinning steel. What is it, physically?

**CLARKE:** It's stored kinetic energy. In a rotating mass. A rotor. Thousands of tonnes, spinning at 3,000 rpm, coupled electromagnetically to every other machine on the network.

**DAWE:** And synthetic inertia?

**CLARKE:** It's a control loop in firmware.

*Long pause.*

**DAWE:** You replaced thousands of tonnes of spinning steel with a control loop in firmware.

**CLARKE:** We *supplemented the transition* with sophisticated digital response capabilities, yes.

**DAWE:** And the spinning steel?

**CLARKE:** Retired. Coal plants, mostly. Some gas. Then the older nuclear. All retired as part of the net zero programme.

**DAWE:** Did you replace it with new spinning steel?

**CLARKE:** We procured synchronous condensers through the Stability Pathfinder. About 12 gigavolt-ampere-seconds.

**DAWE:** You lost 162 and replaced 12.

**CLARKE:** The remainder was covered by ancillary service contracts.

**DAWE:** For the firmware.

**CLARKE:** For the sophisticated digital response capabilities, yes.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** Now. There's a concept I want to ask you about. Rate of change of frequency. RoCoF.

**CLARKE:** Yes.

**DAWE:** In the 1990s, what was the maximum permitted rate of change of frequency?

**CLARKE:** 0.125 hertz per second. That was the protection coordination design point.

**DAWE:** And in 2025, before the event?

**CLARKE:** 1.0 hertz per second. It had been raised progressively.

**DAWE:** Eight times higher.

**CLARKE:** Eight times more *permissive*, yes.

**DAWE:** Did the grid get eight times more resilient?

**CLARKE:** The grid got eight times less inertia. The threshold was adjusted to reflect that.

**DAWE:** So you kept raising the limit to match how bad things had got.

**CLARKE:** We kept *revising the operating envelope* to reflect the *current system configuration*.

**DAWE:** And the current system configuration on the day of the event -

**CLARKE:** Was consistent with our published operating parameters.

**DAWE:** The published operating parameters that produced an eleven-hour blackout.

**CLARKE:** Those parameters, yes.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** The inquiry found that protection relays at distribution level were still set for the 1990s thresholds.

**CLARKE:** Some of them had not been updated, yes. There are a great many protection relays. Updating them all is -

**DAWE:** A large job.

**CLARKE:** An ongoing programme of coordinated asset management, yes. We'd started.

**DAWE:** How far had you got?

**CLARKE:** We'd done quite a thorough review. Excellent documentation.

**DAWE:** But not the relays.

**CLARKE:** The documentation was very thorough.

*Clarke looks at his folder approvingly.*

**DAWE:** So the relays were set for a grid that no longer existed, protecting a grid that no longer had enough inertia for the relays' assumptions to hold, running on synthetic inertia that couldn't arrive in time, with a rate-of-change-of-frequency limit that had been raised eight times to accommodate the deterioration.

**CLARKE:** That's one way of summarising it.

**DAWE:** Is there another way?

*Pause.*

**CLARKE:** We had excellent documentation.

# PART THREE: "The Governance"

*INT. THE SAME ROOM. THREE FOLDERS NOW ON THE TABLE. ALL EMPTY. DAWE LOOKS TIRED.*

---

**DAWE:** Now. The inquiry found something interesting about who was responsible for preventing this.

**CLARKE:** The inquiry found that accountability was *appropriately distributed* across the regulatory framework, yes.

**DAWE:** What does that mean?

**CLARKE:** It means BEIS set the policy.

**DAWE:** And Ofgem?

**CLARKE:** Ofgem regulated the procurement framework.

**DAWE:** And NESO?

**CLARKE:** NESO operated within the framework that Ofgem regulated, under the policy that BEIS set.

**DAWE:** So who was responsible?

**CLARKE:** Collectively, all three. Which in practice means -

**DAWE:** None of them.

**CLARKE:** The accountability was distributed to an optimal level of granularity, yes.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** There was a document. The Ofgem Review of GB Energy System Operation. 2021.

**CLARKE:** An excellent document. Very thorough.

**DAWE:** It proposed separating the system operator from the transmission owner.

**CLARKE:** For independence. Yes.

**DAWE:** Did it propose maintaining a minimum level of synchronous inertia?

**CLARKE:** It proposed a *market framework* for stability services.

**DAWE:** Is that the same thing?

**CLARKE:** It's better, in theory. More flexible.

**DAWE:** The word "inertia" - how many times does it appear in the document?

**CLARKE:** I'd have to check. Not very many.

**DAWE:** Once. Glancingly.

**CLARKE:** It's a governance document. Not a physics document.

**DAWE:** The grid is a physics problem.

**CLARKE:** The *governance of the grid* is a governance problem.

**DAWE:** And when the governance failed?

**CLARKE:** The physics stepped in.

**DAWE:** And the physics said -

**CLARKE:** The physics said eleven hours, yes.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** Now. Stability. The inquiry used a distinction I'd like to understand. Between stability and manageability.

**CLARKE:** A subtle but important distinction.

**DAWE:** Explain it to me.

**CLARKE:** Manageability is the property of being able to see everything, report on everything, procure services against everything. Dashboard. Metrics. Quarterly reviews. Very good accountability structures.

**DAWE:** And stability?

**CLARKE:** Stability is the property of the grid not falling over when something goes wrong.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** Are they the same thing?

**CLARKE:** We believed so. For some time.

**DAWE:** The inquiry found they weren't.

**CLARKE:** The inquiry found they were *related* but *not equivalent*. Which in hindsight seems -

**DAWE:** Obvious.

**CLARKE:** We prefer "not fully anticipated in the framework."

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** The inquiry also found that the previous British grid - before the transition - was stable for reasons that had nothing to do with governance.

**CLARKE:** It was stable because of the rotating machines, yes. The spinning steel. Tens of thousands of tonnes of coupled electromagnetic mass, holding the frequency through sheer physical presence.

**DAWE:** Without being told to.

**CLARKE:** Without being told anything. It just did it. By existing.

**DAWE:** And you replaced that with firmware and quarterly procurement windows.

**CLARKE:** We *transitioned* to a *flexible, digital, market-responsive framework*, yes.

**DAWE:** And the framework was excellent.

**CLARKE:** The framework was exemplary. ISO 55000 compliant. RIIO-T2 aligned. Full stakeholder engagement.

**DAWE:** Until the physics disagreed.

**CLARKE:** Until the physics expressed a *divergent view*, yes.

*Long pause.*

**DAWE:** What happens now?

**CLARKE:** We are undertaking a comprehensive review.

**DAWE:** Of?

**CLARKE:** Of the framework. To ensure that future frameworks are more robustly designed to prevent the kind of outcomes the current framework produced.

**DAWE:** You're going to write a new document.

**CLARKE:** A very thorough document, yes.

**DAWE:** About the importance of spinning steel.

**CLARKE:** About the importance of *intrinsic physical properties underpinning the digital operational layer*, yes.

**DAWE:** Things that exist in the iron whether you've procured them or not.

**CLARKE:** Whether you've *recognised their value in the market framework* or not, yes.

**DAWE:** Things that respond to a disturbance before your firmware has finished reading the question.

**CLARKE:** Before the measurement window has closed, technically, yes.

*Pause.*

**DAWE:** How long will the review take?

**CLARKE:** We're hoping to have interim findings by Q3.

**DAWE:** Of which year?

*Clarke considers this seriously.*

**CLARKE:** We haven't fully scoped the timeline yet. The stakeholder engagement phase alone -

**DAWE:** The lights went out for eleven hours because you replaced spinning steel with spreadsheets, and the response is more spreadsheets.

**CLARKE:** More *sophisticated and better-governed* spreadsheets, yes. With clearer accountability matrices.

*Very long pause.*

**DAWE:** One last thing. Ofgem described the new framework - the one before the event - as "smart, flexible, and future-ready."

**CLARKE:** Yes.

**DAWE:** And the old framework - the spinning steel, the coupled electromagnetic mass, the 260 gigavolt-ampere-seconds of physical inertia?

**CLARKE:** That was considered legacy infrastructure. Inflexible. Carbon-adjacent. Dumb.

**DAWE:** The dumb system kept the lights on for seventy years.

**CLARKE:** It did, yes.

**DAWE:** The smart system lasted until a wind farm had a bad afternoon.

**CLARKE:** The smart system had excellent metrics right up until that point.

*Clarke nods, entirely sincerely.*

**DAWE:** Thank you for your time.

**CLARKE:** Not at all. And I should say - Ofgem has issued a very strongly worded statement expressing full confidence in the process going forward.

**DAWE:** Has anyone actually fixed the inertia?

*Long pause.*

**CLARKE:** We've issued a tender.

**DAWE:** For spinning steel?

**CLARKE:** For "intrinsic synchronous stabilisation assets with grid-forming capability."

**DAWE:** That's spinning steel.

**CLARKE:** It's spinning steel, yes.

**DAWE:** Like you had before.

**CLARKE:** Like we had before.

**DAWE:** That you retired.

**CLARKE:** That we transitioned away from, yes.

*Clarke and Dawe look at each other for a long moment.*

**DAWE:** How's the tender going?

**CLARKE:** Early stages. We're currently in the stakeholder engagement phase.

*Clarke straightens his folder. It remains empty.*
